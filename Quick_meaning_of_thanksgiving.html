Have you ever thought about the irony of Black Friday? Every year, we drop what we’re doing and come home to celebrate the holiday of giving thanks on the last Thursday of November. We come home to see our loved ones; our family and friends who we don’t always have the chance to see frequently. As Thanksgiving dinners conclude, hearts full of gratitude and bellies laden with abundance, a stark irony unfolds with the arrival of Black Friday. In this annual retail frenzy, the spirit of gratitude seems to yield to a consumerist fever where the primary focus shifts from appreciating what we have to acquiring more. Even though Black Friday became a shopping culture in America from as far back as it could be recalled, the idea of it is undeniably contradictory to that of Thanksgiving and the irony of it must not be ignored or overlooked. An even greater irony is that more Americans today identify the day after Thanksgiving as Black Friday than as National Native American Heritage Day.
